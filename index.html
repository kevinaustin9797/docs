What Is CoSpaces Edu?

CoSpaces Edu is a browser-based platform that lets teachers and students build their own Augmented and Virtual Reality (AR and VR) environments without specialized hardware. Using a drag-and-drop interface, teacher candidates can layer 3D objects, text, audio, and interactive scripts over printed materials or classroom objects — all viewable on a smartphone or tablet.

🌐 Browser-Based

No downloads required. Runs on Google Chrome, Mac OS Safari, and most modern browsers.

📱 Device Flexible

AR experiences work on iOS and Android. VR mode supports Google Cardboard, a $10 tool to be included with the lab.

🏫 LMS-Friendly

Scenes can be published and shared via link, is also easy to embed in Canvas or other LMS's.

💰 Free Tier Available

Teachers can get a free class license with up to 30 student accounts.

Why Teachers should use AR in the classroom.

Research tells us that immersive, interactive learning environments improve retention and learner confidence. When students engage actively, rather than passively, they build deeper understanding (Elmqaddem, 2019). AR bridges the gap between abstract content and tangible experience.

Click each principle below to learn how it applies to CoSpaces Edu lesson design.

1

Contextual Anchoring

Layer digital content directly onto physical objects or locations.

In CoSpaces: Attach a 3D model of a cell to a printed textbook diagram. When students point their device at the page, the model appears in context — linking biology concepts to a physical object.
›
2

Learner Control

Let students explore content at their own pace and path.

In CoSpaces: Program branching interactions using CoBlocks. Students can click on objects to reveal different historical perspectives, choose their own investigation path, or trigger audio narrations.
›
3

Multimodal Representation

Deliver content through text, audio, 3D visuals, and interaction simultaneously.

In CoSpaces: Combine labeled 3D objects, embedded audio explanations, and clickable information panels in one scene. This supports multiple learning modalities and benefits students with diverse learning needs, including English learners and students with reading disabilities (Barroso-Osuna et al., 2019).
›
4

Formative Feedback

Build in low-stakes checkpoints to reinforce understanding.

In CoSpaces: Use CoBlocks to create simple embedded quizzes or "unlock" sequences — a student must answer a question correctly before the next 3D object appears. This encourages active recall without the pressure of formal assessment.

Building Your First AR Scene: A 5-Step Workflow

Use this workflow when designing any CoSpaces Edu AR experience for your classroom. Each step maps to a core instructional design consideration.

1

Identify the Anchor Trigger

Choose the physical object (printed image, poster, worksheet) that will "trigger" your AR content. Keep it simple — high-contrast, flat images work best.

2

Define One Clear Objective

Limit each scene to one measurable learning goal. Cognitive overload is the most common design failure in AR experiences. One scene, one concept.

3

Select Assets from the CoSpaces Library

Use the built-in 3D asset library for characters, environments, and objects. Upload custom images or audio for subject-specific content (e.g., primary source documents for social studies).

4

Script Interactions with CoBlocks

Add interactivity using CoBlocks — even beginners can program "on click" events that reveal information, play audio, or display quizzes. Aim for at least two learner interactions per scene.

5

Test for Accessibility

Preview on both iOS and Android. Ensure all audio has text alternatives. Confirm text is readable at arm's length. Check that interactive targets are large enough for diverse motor abilities.

Scenario Check: Apply What You Know

Work through these three scenarios. Select the best instructional decision for each situation.

Scenario 1 of 3

A teacher candidate wants to create a CoSpaces scene about the American Revolution for 5th graders. She plans to include 12 interactive objects, 3 audio clips, an embedded video, and a quiz — all in one scene. What should she reconsider?

✅ Correct! This is too much for one scene. Cognitive overload is a leading design failure in AR. One scene, one objective keeps the experience focused and effective.
❌ Not quite. More content in a single scene increases cognitive load, which research shows decreases retention. Simplify and focus on one core concept per scene.

Scenario 2 of 3

A teacher candidate's AR scene includes important vocabulary labels displayed in small, light-gray text on a white background. A student with low vision raises her hand because she cannot read the labels on her tablet. Which design principle was overlooked?

✅ Correct! Accessibility must be tested before deployment. CoSpaces text should use high-contrast colors and readable font sizes viewable at arm's length on a mobile screen.
❌ Not quite. The issue is accessibility. Low-contrast, small text excludes learners with visual impairments. Step 5 of the design workflow requires checking text readability and contrast before sharing.

Scenario 3 of 3

A teacher candidate wants students to explore the parts of a plant cell at their own pace. He is deciding between: (A) a single, linear slide presentation, or (B) a CoSpaces scene with clickable 3D cell organelles that each reveal a description and audio explanation. Which better supports the design principle of learner agency?

✅ Correct! Interactive, branching experiences give learners control over their path through content — a key advantage of AR tools like CoSpaces over linear presentations.
❌ Not quite. Learner agency means students choose their own path. A linear slide forces a single sequence; the CoSpaces scene lets students click on what interests them and explore independently.
